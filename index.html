01 · The setting

The three-week window between April 5 and April 27.

Opening

Israel Tech Week runs five days, nine vertical summits, 20 curated events, and 2,000 expected attendees, anchored to The LAB Miami and dovetailed with eMerge Americas. The core team is small. The sponsor roster stretches from Diamond through vertical-specific banking, legal, accounting, and defense partners.

Between Showcase application close on April 5 and doors opening April 27, a three-week window has to absorb hundreds of applicant decks, nine parallel programming streams, and the cross-summit matchmaking that determines whether Israeli founders leave Miami with real US and Latin American relationships.

Most of that work is not an AI problem. It is a structured-context problem dressed up as an AI problem.
02 · The frame

A rough split of where each layer of the work actually lives.

60 / 30 / 10

Eduba's working view of most operational stacks is that roughly 60% of the workload is traditional code and database work, 30% is rule-based logic, and the remaining 10% is where language models actually earn their seat.

60%
Code & database

Sponsor fulfillment tracking. Attendee data. Post-event reporting. This is Postgres work, not AI work.

30%
Rule-based logic

Summit scheduling against room capacity. Cross-vertical conflict checks. Deterministic routing.

10%
Language models

Reading hundreds of Showcase applications, extracting committee-relevant signals, generating defensible scoring rationales for human override.

Applied to a conference operation: sponsor fulfillment tracking is database work, not AI. Summit scheduling against room capacity is rule-based logic, not AI. Reading hundreds of Showcase applications, extracting the handful of signals that matter to the committee, and generating defensible scoring rationales the committee can review and override, that is the 10% where a properly-scoped language model pays for itself in a single cycle.

The value of drawing those lines is not faster tooling. It is fewer wrong tools.
